AI Playbook


In this session, we'll ask you to walk us through key defining features of a system you are designing. These features will determine various types of scenarios that your users might encounter as they interact with your system. This playbook will guide you through some of these scenarios and help you understand several techniques that you and your team can consider including in user testing.

You will be asked to complete a total of two tasks. In each task, you will answer a set of multiple choice questions (up to 7) related to your design of a feature or system. You will be provided with tips and examples in the help center to help guide your selection of choices. As you are answering the questions, you will also see a preview of a list of scenarios that you could potetially include in user testing. Once you have completed answering the questions, you will see the full list of scenarios and recommendations for simulating the expected outcomes (including errors) of an AI system given each scenario.


Task 1 (5 min)

Design a simple chatbot system that will help its users find a rental apartment that best suits their preference.


Task 2 (10 min)

Design a feature or system that you will work on. Here are some things to keep in mind while completing this task:

  • Pick an idea that you and your team have just began to conceptualize.
  • You can also start with a project that you recently worked on, but has not yet reached full integration with AI.
  • Please use this Playbook as if it is now part of your practice during early prototyping or design phases.

 Search

A search system is a program or feature that searches for and identifies items in collection that correspond to keywords, phrases, or characters specified by the user.

 Example

  • Search engines such as Bing generate matching results (e.g., movie title) along with contextually relevant information (e.g., showtimes) based on the user's current location and time.
  • An in-app search feature such as Microsoft Search in office products allows users to quickly find application specific information, from commands, help, to recently used files and more.

 Recommendation

A recommendation system suggests content to the user that is likely to be of interest. In some cases, this is done by predicting the rating or preference a user would give to an item.

 Example

  • Playlist generators for video and music services like Netflix, YouTube and Spotify.
  • Product recommender services such as Amazon.
  • Contextual assistant services such as Meeting Insights

 Conversational AI

Conversational agents, or dialogue systems, interact with people through natural-language dialogue. Historically this is achieved by recognizing speech, classifying intents, and 'slot filling'.

 Example

  • Voice assistants for productivity management or home automation like Cortana, Siri, and Alexa.
  • Chatbots for customer support in most ecommerce websites.

 Text Prediction and Assistance

Text prediction and writing assistants utilize machine learning to help users through various steps of the writing process, including research, grammar and tone checking, and localization.

 Example

  • Predicting the next set of words or action commands based on the user's input.
  • Providing corrections for spelling, grammar, and punctuation mistakes.
  • Making suggestions for similar words, paraphrases, and alternative expressions.

 Classification

Text or document classification refers to the process of categorizing text into organized groups by assigning predefined labels to them.

 Example

  • Spam filters in email applications such as Microsoft Outlook.
  • Scheduling assistants that detect meeting appointments based on the content of email.

 Single Response

Your system will produce a single response if there is only one valid response available, or if there are time, screen size, usability related constraints that only allow the system to produce one response.

 Example

Advanced chatbot systems can generate short blurbs of text in interactive conversations by building on the tone and relevant topics that are detected from user's utterances.

 Example

A single response to a question related to weather might include:

  • It is 81 degrees Fahrenheit.

 Example

Advanced writing assistants often generate a single response to suggest a paraphrase or sentence that eliminates a grammatical error or redundant information.

 Ranked list

Your system will produce a ranked list if there are multiple valid responses available. Items in a ranked list are presented in order of items with the highest contextual relevance to the user's needs.

 Action execution

Performing an action is a common response type for systems that generate nonverbal cues.

 Example

  • Displaying a screen or opening a new application.
  • Performing physical actions such as emitting light or vibrotactile feedback.

Spelling error results in wrong input


Spelling mistakes are a common source of text input errors, including missing letters, accidental letter additions, swapping and replacing letters. Here are some ways to simulate spelling errors:

  • Use a word scrambler to switch the position of any two adjacent letters.
  • Use any of the four techniques to manipulate the user's utterance.
    • Truncation: removing a vowel or consonant from a word (e.g., leter instead of letter)
    • Substitution: replacing a vowel or consonant with another one in their respective position in the word (e.g., fimite instead of finite)
    • Insertion: adding an extra vowel or consonant to a word (e.g., misstake instead of mistake)
    • Swapping: switching positions between two adjacent letters (e.g., recieved instead of received)

Speech recognition error results in wrong input


Speech recognition is often a common source of error in technologies that rely on language intelligence. Here are some ways to simulate speech recognition errors:

  • Use an automated speech-to-text transcriber to convert user's utterance to text.
  • Use any of the four techniques to manipulate the user's utterance.
    • Truncation: removing inflected endings (e.g., "-ing", "-s", "-ed") or articles ("the")
    • Substitution: replacing utterance with a homo-phone/-nym, or words that share the same pronunciation, regardless of spelling.
    • Insertion: adding inflected endings (e.g., "-ing", "-s", "-ed")
    • Extension Splitting inflected endings ("-s") from words ("cats") to add a new word (e.g., cats -> cat sit)
